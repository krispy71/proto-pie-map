# Human Civilization & PIE Research Report (April 2026)

## Pre-Historic Human Development and Migration (300,000–10,000 BCE)

### 1. Human Origins in Africa (300,000–70,000 BCE)
*   **Anatomically Modern Humans:** The discovery of fossils at **Jebel Irhoud, Morocco**, dated to ~315,000 BP (Hublin et al., 2017), has fundamentally shifted the timeline of *Homo sapiens* emergence. This supports a **Pan-African mosaic model**, suggesting that our species evolved across a network of interacting populations across the continent rather than a single East African "Eden" (Skoglund & Mathieson, 2018).
*   **Behavioral Modernity:** Middle Stone Age (MSA) sites exhibit the gradual emergence of modern cognitive traits. Key evidence includes ochre processing, shell beads, and abstract engravings at **Blombos Cave** (~75kya) and **Pinnacle Point** (Marean et al., 2007).
*   **Contemporaneous Hominins:** Modern humans co-existed with other lineages, including *Homo heidelbergensis*, the small-brained *Homo naledi* (Rising Star Cave), and *Homo rhodesiensis*, likely participating in complex demographic interactions.

### 2. Archaic Human Species and Admixture
*   **Neanderthals:** Ranging across Europe and Western Asia, Neanderthals possessed sophisticated Mousterian technology and complex symbolic behaviors before their extinction ~40,000 BCE (Prüfer et al., 2014).
*   **Denisovans:** Fragmentary remains from **Denisova Cave** and the **Xiahe mandible** (Tibet) reveal a lineage adapted to vast geographic ranges, including high altitudes. They contributed ~4–6% of the genome to modern Melanesian and Australasian populations (Reich et al., 2010).
*   **Island Hominins:** Discoveries of *Homo floresiensis* (~100–50 kya) on Flores and *Homo luzonensis* (~50–67 kya) in the Philippines demonstrate "island dwarfism" and the survival of isolated archaic lineages late into the Pleistocene.
*   **Introgression:** Non-African modern humans carry ~1.5–2.1% Neanderthal DNA, with retained variants influencing immune systems and environmental adaptations (skin/hair).

### 3. Out-of-Africa Dispersal(s) (70,000–50,000 BCE)
*   **The 'Great Expansion':** Genomic data supports a major dispersal ~60,000 BCE that accounts for the majority of non-African ancestry, following a severe genetic bottleneck (Mallick et al., 2016; Pagani et al., 2016).
*   **Earlier Pulses:** Pre-60kya migrations reached the Levant (**Misliya Cave**, ~180kya), Europe (**Apidima**, ~210kya; Harvati et al., 2019), and potentially China (**Daoxian**, ~80–120kya), though these likely left no descendants in living populations.
*   **Routes:** Dispersals followed a **Southern Route** via coastal Arabia and the Indian subcontinent (supported by shell middens and haplogroup M distribution) and a **Northern Route** via the Levant corridor.
*   **Demographics:** The founding non-African population is estimated to have been as small as 1,000–10,000 effective individuals.

### 4. Peopling of Eurasia (70,000–40,000 BCE)
*   **South Asia:** Earliest modern human sites appear post-Toba (~74,000 BCE); current debates focus on whether humans survived the supervolcano in the Indian subcontinent (Petraglia et al., 2007).
*   **East Asia:** Significant remains include **Tianyuan Man** (~40kya; Fu et al., 2013) and the **Tam Pa Ling** skull in Laos (~46–63kya).
*   **Southeast Asia & Wallacea:** Arrival required sea crossings even at lowest sea levels, implying the use of watercraft by ~65,000 BCE.
*   **Europe:** The earliest confirmed modern humans in Europe are found at **Bacho Kiro Cave**, Bulgaria (~45,000 BP; Hublin et al., 2020), associated with the Initial Upper Paleolithic and eventual Neanderthal replacement over ~5,000 years.

### 5. Peopling of Australia (65,000–40,000 BCE)
*   **Madjedbebe (Northern Territory):** OSL dating confirms human occupation by ~65,000 BP (Clarkson et al., 2017), making it the oldest confirmed modern human site outside Africa and Eurasia.
*   **Sahul Crossing:** Entry into the Sahul shelf required a minimum 90 km open-water crossing, indicating intentional and sophisticated seafaring.
*   **Innovation:** Earliest Australian technology includes ground-edge axes and grinding stones that predate similar finds elsewhere by tens of thousands of years.
*   **Highland Domestication:** In New Guinea, independent crop domestication (taro, banana, sugarcane) began as early as ~9,000 BCE.
*   **Genetic Divergence:** Aboriginal Australians and Papuans split ~40,000 BP after their initial shared entry into Sahul.

### 6. Upper Paleolithic Revolution (50,000–12,000 BCE)
*   **Figurative Art:** The era saw an explosion of symbolic expression, from the cave paintings of **Chauvet** (~37,000 BCE) to the **Sulawesi cave art** (Aubert et al., 2019), which includes potentially the oldest figurative art at ~45,500 BP.
*   **Musical Instruments:** The **Hohle Fels vulture-bone flute** (~40,000 BCE; Conard et al., 2009) marks the earliest evidence for complex musical traditions.
*   **Venus Figurines:** Distributed from Iberia to Siberia (~28,000–22,000 BCE), these objects reflect widespread cultural connectivity and shared symbolic systems.
*   **Cultural Successions:** Distinct traditions emerged across Europe, including the Gravettian (28–22 kya), Solutrean (22–17 kya), and Magdalenian (17–12 kya).
*   **Domestication:** Dog domestication likely occurred between 15,000 and 40,000 BCE, with most evidence supporting a single origin from East or Central Asian wolves.

### 7. Americas Entry (20,000–10,000 BCE)
*   **White Sands Footprints:** Definitive evidence of humans in New Mexico ~21,000–23,000 BCE (Bennett et al., 2021) proves occupation during the Last Glacial Maximum (LGM).
*   **Beringian Standstill:** Genetic models suggest a ~10,000-year isolation in Beringia before southward dispersal (Tamm et al., 2007).
*   **Kelp Highway:** A coastal watercraft route along the Pacific kelp forest belt is supported as the primary entry path, predating the inland "Ice-Free Corridor."
*   **Clovis Culture:** Emerging ~13,500 BCE, Clovis technology spread rapidly across the continent within 500 years.
*   **Genetic Makeup:** Americas populations derive from a single founding group from Northeast Asia (Raghavan et al., 2015), with some Amazonian groups showing a distinct Australasian signal (Skoglund et al., 2015).

### 8. Late Pleistocene Climate and Human Adaptation
*   **Last Glacial Maximum (LGM):** Between ~22,000–18,000 BCE, massive ice sheets covered much of the Northern Hemisphere. Humans retreated into refugia in Southern Europe, East Asia, and Africa.
*   **Climate Oscillations:** Rapid shifts known as Heinrich Events and Dansgaard-Oeschger cycles caused frequent population fragmentation and replacement.
*   **Younger Dryas:** An abrupt cooling (~10,800–9,700 BCE) caused the collapse of Clovis culture and triggered the **Natufian** transition to sedentary living and early agriculture in the Levant.
*   **Recolonization:** Post-glacial warming (~11,700 BCE) led to rapid northward expansion and the establishment of Holocene population structures.

## Codebase Audit

### Data Accuracy Issues
- **Anatolian Paradox**: Current `data.js` and `ROADMAP.md` describe the Anatolian branch splitting from the steppe. Recent (2024-2025) research (Lazaridis, Reich) suggests a **two-stage model** where the Anatolian branch split from a **Caucasus-Lower Volga (CLV)** population *before* it mixed with Ukrainian hunter-gatherers to form the Yamnaya.
- **Genetic Proportions**: Some admixture values in `data.js` are slightly dated. For example, Yamnaya is now modeled as a 4/5ths CLV and 1/5th UNHG (Ukraine Neolithic Hunter-Gatherer) mix.
- **Sredny Stog Dates**: Currently starts at -4000 BCE in `data.js`; consensus now supports -4500 BCE for the earliest Eneolithic steppe formations.
- **Tocharian Migration**: The eastward migration of Afanasievo is well-captured, but the "Tocharian" entry endpoint (900 CE) should be noted as the terminal phase of the language in the Tarim Basin following Uyghur expansion.

### Missing Cultures / Migrations
- **Pre-PIE Substrates**: Missing explicit entries for **WHG** (Western Hunter-Gatherer), **EHG** (Eastern), **CHG** (Caucasus), and **ANF** (Anatolian Neolithic Farmer). These are the fundamental building blocks of the IE gene pool.
- **The "Southern Arc"**: No representation of the West Asian / Caucasus population that contributed the CHG component to the steppe.
- **Missing Branches**:
  - **Baltic**: Corded Ware Baltic and subsequent Baltic cultures are entirely absent.
  - **Albanian**: Illyrian and Paleo-Balkan precursors are missing.
  - **Slavic**: Prague-Penkov culture (~500–700 CE) is missing.
- **Intermediaries**: **BMAC** (Bactria-Margiana) is a critical missing link for the Indo-Aryan migration into South Asia.

### Code Quality Notes
- **Security**: Excellent use of `.textContent` and a custom `escapeHTML` utility to prevent XSS. `server.js` implements `helmet` (CSP), `morgan` (IP anonymization), and `express-rate-limit`.
- **Visualization**: `app.js` is robust. It correctly handles interpolation and supports both circle and ellipse modes.
- **Roadmap Alignment**: The code is well-prepared for the features listed in `ROADMAP.md`, such as URL state (already partially implemented) and site markers.

## Historical Research

### Human Origins and Early Migration Out of Africa
- **Multiple Dispersals**: Consensus has shifted from a "single event" to a **Multiple Dispersals** framework.
- **Timeline**: *Homo sapiens* emerged across Africa ~300,000 years ago (Jebel Irhoud). Early "failed" dispersals occurred ~210kya (Greece) and ~180kya (Israel).
- **The "Great Expansion"**: The successful migration occurred **50,000–60,000 years ago**.
- **The "Hub" Model**: Genetic evidence suggests humans stayed in a "hub" (likely the Persian Plateau) for ~30,000 years before radiating into Europe and Asia.

### Pre-PIE Ancestral Populations (Eurasia)
- **The CLV Population**: The **Caucasus-Lower Volga (CLV)** cline (c. 4500 BCE) is now identified as the ancestor of both the Anatolian branch and Core-PIE.
- **Genetic Components**:
  - **WHG**: Blue-eyed, dark-skinned hunter-gatherers of Europe.
  - **ANF**: Near Eastern farmers who brought agriculture to Europe (~6000 BCE).
  - **EHG/CHG**: Mixed on the steppe to form the PIE genetic foundation.

### PIE Homeland and the Yamnaya
- **Two-Stage Model**:
  - **Stage 1**: Proto-Indo-Anatolian origin in the **Southern Arc** (West Asia/Caucasus).
  - **Stage 2**: Dispersal of all other branches from the **Pontic-Caspian Steppe** (Yamnaya) c. 3000 BCE.
- **Yamnaya Legacy**: Responsible for ~75% genetic replacement in Northern Europe (Corded Ware) and significant impact in South Asia.

### Bronze Age Dispersal & Language Branch Timelines
- **Order of Split**: Anatolian (4200 BC) → Tocharian (3700 BC) → Italo-Celtic/Germanic (3000 BC) → Balto-Slavic/Indo-Iranian (2500-2200 BC).
- **Technological Vectors**: The spread was facilitated by **horse domestication** (Don-Volga, 2200 BCE) and the **spoked-wheel chariot** (Sintashta).

### Near East and Africa
- **Mesopotamia**: Sumerian urbanism (Uruk), Akkadian empire-building, and Babylonian legal systems. The **4.2ka event** (megadrought) is now seen as a primary driver of societal collapse.
- **Ancient Egypt**: Rapid unification c. 3100 BCE. New Kingdom chronology is being refined (starting later c. 1530 BCE).
- **Bantu Expansion**: Started 6,000–4,000 years ago in West-Central Africa. A **"serial-founder"** model confirms rapid spread through rainforests using high adaptability before iron tools became dominant.

### South Asia
- **Indus Valley (IVC)**: Now confirmed to be **non-steppe** (no Yamnaya DNA).
- **Vedic Transition**: Indo-Aryan migration (1900–1500 BCE) occurred *after* the IVC's climate-driven decline.
- **Genetic Makeup**: Modern South Asians are a three-way mix of IVC-related, Steppe (Vedic), and Ancient Ancestral South Indian (AASI) populations.

### East Asia
- **Yellow River**: Sophisticated urbanism at **Nanzuo** (Yangshao) and "multigrid" planning at **Erlitou** (Xia/Shang prototype).
- **Shang/Zhou**: Highly centralized state resource management and the development of the "central axis" architectural tradition.

### Oceania and Austronesian Expansion
- **Out-of-Taiwan**: Consistently supported. Migration started c. 5,000 years ago.
- **Lapita Culture**: Emerged c. 1500 BCE. Seafarers introduced pigs, dogs, and chickens to New Guinea and settled the Pacific "pulse and pause" style.
- **Aboriginal Australians**: 65,000 years of continuity. Genetic diversity is among the highest globally, reflecting deep regional structure.

### The Americas
- **Coastal Route First**: Primary entry via the **Kelp Highway** 16,000–18,000 years ago.
- **Timeline Extremes**: White Sands footprints (21,000–23,000 years ago) push the timeline back significantly.
- **Monte Verde (2026 Update)**: Recent re-evaluation suggests the site may be younger than previously thought, reigniting debates over the "Clovis First" inland route.
- **Amazon Basin**: LiDAR has revealed a **"cultural parkland"** with 2,500-year-old urban metropolises (Upano Valley) and intentionally engineered **Terra Preta** soil covering 3% of the basin.

### Key Academic Sources
- **Haak et al. (2015)**: Steppe migration into Europe.
- **Narasimhan et al. (2019)**: Formation of South Asian populations.
- **Lazaridis et al. (2022/2024)**: The Southern Arc and CLV models.
- **Librado et al. (2021)**: Horse domestication origins.
- **Nakagome et al. (2025)**: Maya genetic continuity.
- **Hünemeier et al. (2025)**: Mesoamerican "third wave" migrations.
- **Spinney (2024)**: General synthesis of PIE dispersal.

## Gemini Audit — PIE Language Group Logic (2026-05-01)

**1. How are language branches modeled? Required vs optional fields?**
Language branches are modeled as an object (`branches`) in the dataset where each key is a unique branch identifier (e.g., `celtic`), and the value is an object containing `name`, `color` (fill), and `textColor`. Both `name` and `color` are required for rendering map shapes, arrows, and legend buttons. `textColor` appears optional as `app.js` does not actively use it for map rendering.

**2. How do cultures reference branches? What happens if a branch key is missing?**
Cultures reference a branch via the `branch` string property (e.g., `branch: 'celtic'`). If a culture references a branch key that does not exist in the `branches` dictionary, `app.js` will crash with a fatal error (`TypeError: Cannot read properties of undefined (reading 'color')`) when attempting to generate the territory shape in `updateTerritories`.

**3. How does branchVisible filtering work in buildLegend and renderYear?**
In `buildLegend`, a boolean dictionary `this.branchVisible` is initialized with all dataset branches set to `true`. Clicking a legend button toggles its branch's boolean state and triggers `renderYear()`. 
In `renderYear`, `updateTerritories` and `updateMigrations` evaluate `this.branchVisible[culture.branch]` (or `mig.branch`). If `false`, `updateTerritories` explicitly removes the culture's circle and label layers from the map, while `updateMigrations` sets the migration polyline and arrow opacities to `0`.

**4. What does a new dataset need to be fully DATASETS-registry compatible?**
A fully compatible dataset must include:
- `startYear` and `endYear` (numbers).
- `meta` object with `timelineMin`, `timelineMax`, `defaultYear`, `title`, and `subtitle`.
- `branches` object mapping keys to `{ name, color }`.
- `cultures` array where each object has `{ id, name, branch, description, startYear, endYear, phases }`. The `phases` array must contain at least one phase with `{ year, center: [lat, lon], radius }`.
- `migrations` array where each object has `{ id, name, branch, description, startYear, endYear, animateStart, animateEnd, path }`.
- `events` array (if missing, the `for (const ev of events)` loop in `updateEventTicker` will throw an error).
- `genetics` object mapping branch keys to description strings (if missing entirely, `this.data.genetics[culture.branch]` will throw a TypeError in `showInfo`).
- `sites` and `sources` arrays (can be omitted or empty, checked safely in `app.js`).

**5. Are there any hardcoded PIE-specific assumptions in app.js that would break a non-PIE dataset?**
Yes, the admixture bar chart relies on hardcoded PIE/European genetic components. In `app.js`, `PIEMigrationMap.ADMIXTURE_COMPONENTS` is statically defined with keys: `EHG`, `CHG`, `WHG`, `ANF`, `IranN`, and `Other`. A non-PIE dataset using different genetic keys (e.g., AASI for South Asia, Denisovan) would fail to render in the admixture bars because the rendering loop only checks for these hardcoded keys.

**6. What would a new language family dataset (Afro-Asiatic, Sino-Tibetan, Niger-Congo) need?**
To support a new language family seamlessly:
- A new data file structured with the required schema (`branches`, `cultures`, `migrations`, `events`, `genetics`).
- An update to `app.js` to decouple `ADMIXTURE_COMPONENTS` from PIE specifics. The dataset should ideally provide its own `admixtureComponents` color mapping, or `app.js` needs to dynamically build the admixture legend based on the keys present in the data. Otherwise, new datasets must either omit the `admixture` field or place everything under `Other`.

**7. Note schema inconsistencies between PIE_DATA and CIVILIZATIONS_DATA.**
- **Admixture:** `PIE_DATA` cultures include detailed `admixture` proportions. `CIVILIZATIONS_DATA` completely omits the `admixture` field, likely to bypass the hardcoded PIE components in `app.js`.
- **Branch usage:** `PIE_DATA` strictly uses the `branch` concept for linguistic families (e.g., `italic`, `germanic`), while `CIVILIZATIONS_DATA` overloads `branch` to represent geographic regions (e.g., `near_east`, `americas_meso`).
- **Optional fields:** `CIVILIZATIONS_DATA` explicitly provides empty arrays for `sites` and `sources` (`sites: [], sources: []`), whereas `PIE_DATA` fully populates them.
